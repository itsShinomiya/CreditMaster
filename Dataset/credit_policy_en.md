# Credit Policies

## 1. Objective

This document establishes the credit policies applicable to the assessment of loan proposals, based on the criteria used by the project's credit opinion generator. The policy seeks to balance responsible growth, delinquency control, decision consistency, and preservation of portfolio quality.

Credit decisions must be objective, documented, and supported by evidence. No approval should depend on a single positive indicator when there are relevant signs of deterioration in repayment capacity or credit behavior.

## 2. Decision Principles

Decisions must follow five principles:

1. Repayment capacity above commercial appetite.
2. Credit history as the central signal of future behavior.
3. Debt burden compatible with recurring income.
4. Exposure proportional to the risk profile.
5. Mandatory mitigants for relevant exceptions.

The credit opinion must state the recommendation, risk level, strengths, risk factors, and executive conclusion. Missing data must not be presumed positive.

## 3. FICO Score Classification

The FICO score must be classified according to the ranges below:

| Minimum FICO | Classification | Guideline |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 760 and above | Very strong | Eligible for approval, provided the other indicators are aligned. |
| 720 to 759 | Strong | Relevant strength for approval. |
| 680 to 719 | Adequate | Acceptable, subject to good repayment capacity. |
| 640 to 679 | Moderate | Requires greater caution and objective compensating factors. |
| Below 640 | Weak | Restrictive indicator; approve only with strong mitigants and higher authority. |
| Not provided | No score provided | Treat as a risk factor until documented evidence is provided. |

Scores below 660 must be considered a relevant negative factor. Scores from 720 onward must be considered a strength.

## 4. Repayment Capacity and DTI

DTI represents the relationship between financial obligations and income. The policy adopts the following parameters:

| DTI | Classification | Guideline |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Up to 15% | Controlled | Strength. |
| Above 15% up to 30% | Acceptable | Continue the analysis of the other indicators. |
| Above 30% | High | Risk factor; requires compensating factors. |
| Not provided | Not measured | Treat as a restriction until income and debts are validated. |

Proposals with DTI above 30% must not be approved automatically, even if they have a good score. Approval must depend on stable income, low recent delinquency, a requested amount proportional to income, and absence of other stress signals.

## 5. Delinquency History

The existence of delinquencies in the last two years is a priority risk factor. Recent delinquency history indicates behavioral fragility and must weigh more than unverified narrative explanations.

Guidelines:

| Indicator | Treatment |
| --- | --- |
| No delinquencies in the last two years | Strength. |
| One or more delinquencies in the last two years | Relevant risk factor. |
| Missing data | Request evidence or treat as incomplete information. |

When recent delinquency is combined with low FICO, high DTI, or a long term, the default recommendation must be to deny or require strong mitigants.

## 6. Recent Credit Inquiries

Frequent inquiries over a short period may indicate intense demand for liquidity or financial deterioration.

| Inquiries in the last 6 months | Treatment |
| --- | --- |
| 0 to 2 | Acceptable. |
| 3 or more | Risk factor due to recent credit pressure. |

Three or more recent inquiries do not prevent approval by themselves, but they reduce appetite for increased exposure, especially in proposals with high requested amounts, high DTI, or a moderate score.

## 7. Revolving Credit Utilization

Revolving utilization must be used as an indicator of liquidity and financial discipline.

| Revolving utilization | Classification | Guideline |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Up to 35% | Low | Strength. |
| Above 35% and below 80% | Moderate | Acceptable, while observing the other indicators. |
| 80% and above | High | Relevant risk factor. |

Utilization equal to or above 80% must be interpreted as possible dependence on short-term credit.

## 8. Requested Amount Relative to Income

The proposal must be proportional to declared annual income.

| Requested amount / annual income | Treatment |
| --- | --- |
| Up to 20% | Strength. |
| Above 20% up to 45% | Acceptable through integrated analysis. |
| Above 45% | Risk factor due to high exposure. |

Proposals above 45% of annual income must require higher-authority review, amount reduction, additional collateral, or denial.

## 9. Loan Term

The term directly influences risk exposure.

| Term | Guideline |
| --- | --- |
| 36 months | Standard term, lower exposure. |
| 60 months | Long term; increases risk and must be treated as a risk factor. |

Operations with 60-month terms must be approved only when there is consistent repayment capacity and absence of severe restrictions.

## 10. Purpose, Housing Status, and Employment Length

Purpose, housing status, and employment length must contextualize the decision, but they must not replace the quantitative indicators.

Purposes such as debt consolidation and credit card refinancing must receive additional attention when accompanied by high revolving utilization, many recent inquiries, or high DTI.

Housing status and employment length must be used as complementary stability signals. Missing data must not automatically benefit the applicant.

## 11. Recommendation Matrix

The recommendation must follow the integrated assessment of the factors:

| Profile | Expected condition | Recommendation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Low to moderate | Adequate FICO or higher, DTI up to 30%, no recent delinquency, and amount proportional to income | Approve, with regular monitoring. |
| Moderate with concerns | Some risk factors, but with clear compensating factors, such as strong FICO, controlled DTI, or low revolving utilization | Approve with conditions or reduce exposure. |
| High | Low FICO, recent delinquency, high DTI, high revolving utilization, many inquiries, long term, or high amount relative to income | Deny or require strong mitigants. |
| Incomplete | Essential information missing, especially score, income, or DTI | Suspend decision until data remediation. |

## 12. Acceptable Mitigants

When the proposal presents high risk, approval may occur only with proportional mitigants, such as:

- Reduction of the requested amount.
- Reduction of the term.
- Additional evidence of recurring income.
- Prior settlement of short-term debts.
- Additional collateral, when applicable.
- Co-obligor with a stronger credit profile.
- Risk-compatible pricing, when permitted by commercial policy.

Mitigants must not be used to hide structural inability to pay.

## 13. Decision Authority Levels

The authority level must reflect the combined risk of the operation:

| Situation | Recommended authority level |
| --- | --- |
| Low-to-moderate profile with no severe restrictions | Credit analyst. |
| DTI above 30%, 60-month term, or three or more recent inquiries | Credit coordination. |
| FICO below 660, recent delinquency, or amount above 45% of annual income | Credit management. |
| Multiple severe factors or missing essential data | Credit committee or automatic denial. |

Exceptions must be recorded with objective justification, applied mitigants, and the person responsible for approval.

## 14. Credit Opinion Guidelines

Every credit opinion must contain:

- Recommendation: approve, approve with conditions, suspend, or deny.
- Estimated risk: low, low to moderate, moderate, or high.
- Profile summary: FICO, DTI, income, requested amount, purpose, and term.
- Observed strengths.
- Observed risk factors.
- Executive conclusion with decision and conditions, when applicable.

The text must be clear, impersonal, and verifiable. Missing data must not be invented, and objective risk signals must not be softened.

## 15. Governance and Monitoring

The credit area must periodically review:

- Approval rate by FICO range.
- Delinquency by DTI range.
- Loss by operation term.
- Performance of customers with recent inquiries.
- Performance of proposals with high revolving utilization.
- Alignment between credit opinion, decision, and observed outcome.

The parameters of this policy must be recalibrated whenever there is a relevant change in portfolio behavior, the macroeconomic environment, funding cost, risk appetite, or quality of registration information.

## 16. Executive Positioning

As credit leadership, the guidance is clear: portfolio growth is healthy only when supported by real repayment capacity and risk discipline. Approvals must be granted to profiles with verifiable fundamentals, not based on expectations of future recovery.

Profiles with good history, controlled debt burden, and amount proportional to income must have an efficient approval flow. Profiles with recent signs of deterioration, excessive leverage, or incomplete data must be contained, renegotiated, or declined. The consistency of this discipline is what protects the portfolio, the institution, and the customer from poorly granted credit.
